# 3xx out now

![3xx cover art](/images/releases/3xx-cover.jpg)

I still feel some imposter syndrome when I call this an album.

Even though I spent over 120 hours on it, and even though it’s a full album length, there’s a part of me that doesn’t think it “counts.”

### Permission to create

It was my music teacher, Alex, who first told me about Fred Again’s practice of quantity over quality when it comes to the initial stages of songwriting.

For a long time, I thought of creativity as a skill to be mastered. That once I’d reached pro levels of creativity I’d be able to sit down at the piano / guitar / computer / easel, crack my knuckles, and produce a compelling piece of art every time.

I expected periods of dormancy in-between of course - I didn’t think I’d become a prodigy churning out masterpieces daily. But I thought that, if I truly applied the requisite skill, that a quality end result was a sure thing.

But to Fred, the initial stages of creativity are all about creating in bulk, and then later deciding which is the good stuff. So it’s a game of quantity - the more ideas you make, the more chance of having some good ideas in there.

This was recounted on [his episode of Tape Notes](https://soundcloud.com/user-842890129/tn-75-fred-again), where he explained his discovery that a song sketch made in 15 mins captured the essence of the idea just as well as spending 30 mins on the same idea. The other 15 mins he just spent tinkering and tweaking, not really adding anything new (and, sometimes, making it worse).

So rather than spending a couple of hours making 3-4 ideas, he’d instead make 7-8 ideas in the same amount of time. He found that time spent finessing and embellishing one idea is time away from developing something entirely new - potentially missing out on a new great song.

More ideas meant more chance of creating something great.

Since then, I’ve found that this quantity approach to creativity is far more prevalent than I expected. Many artists share this practice: create a lot, then pick out the pieces that speak to them the most. (Examples)

### Sorting through all the ideas

This totally changed my perspective on creative output. I started to make track ideas more often, genuinely not worrying about [whether they were good or not](https://bigecho.co.uk/articles/does-our-creativity-run-out/?utm_source=manualshare).

Naturally this has led to a hard drive full of song ideas. These are usually around 1-2 minutes long, demonstrating a single idea or, sometimes, two complementary parts - like a verse / chorus or an A part / B part.

I recently made my 500th such idea with Ableton, since I bought the software in December 2022.

Now, I know that this is far too many ideas to realistically ever see a ‘proper’ release, and I know that is entirely the point of this approach. However, there are dozens and dozens of these ideas which I feel deserve to be more than just bytes on a hard drive.

So I would semi-regularly listen to these 500 ideas, to see how they felt to me and how that feeling changed with time. I subscribe to Apple Music, which gives me the ability to add my own audio files to my music library and have them sync across my devices. This makes it easy for me to keep all of my song ideas in a playlist which I can occasionally shuffle through when I’m on long drives or walks.

![Screenshot of jams playlist](/images/posts/jams-playlist.png)

It felt good to revisit these ideas, to check in on them, and let them know I hadn’t forgotten about them. In hindsight I can see now that I was subconsciously putting them into 3 buckets as I listened:

1. Ideas that I’d happily never hear again (or, that perhaps I’d come to years from now and appreciate in a new way)
2. Ideas that I was excited to go back to and develop further
3. Ideas in which I recognised potential, and that I didn’t want to abandon, but for which I lacked the energy or inspiration to extend into ‘full’ tracks

Some of the ideas in Bucket #2 have gone on to become singles or collabs. And there are still plenty of ideas in this bucket that I roughly know how I would progress and finish as longer tracks, once I find the time.

But it’s the ideas in Bucket #3 that were niggling at me. The 1-2 min song ideas in this bucket didn’t necessarily represent potential for ‘full’ 3-4 min tracks, but they were still special in their own way. They deserved to be heard.

### What is an album?

Up until this point I never really thought about releasing an album. More than anything, I just wasn’t sure how to put my music into a collection that made the kind of sense that the albums I listened to made. A coherent group of tracks with a similar genre, similar lengths, of a certain ilk.

I was just making stuff and having fun, and then challenging myself to release some of the things I made in small doses.

Then in early 2025 I heard [Skrillex](https://skrillex.com)’s new album, which totally opened my mind to what an album could be.

A collection of 34 tracks, most 1-3 mins long (some under 1 min), blended together in one continuous 46 minute mix.

To me, this style of album simultaneously appeals to a TikTok generation of listeners used to short and sweet dopamine hits, while also encouraging deep listening - with a voiceover on the first track requesting listeners “listen in full with no skips.” The album is truly best enjoyed as a single multi-faceted experience, and I haven’t really been compelled to playlist any of the tracks, because they work so much better as a whole piece.

My comment about attention spans isn’t meant to sound derogatory - I’m acutely familiar with how little bandwidth I have for new information, new perspectives and, sadly, new art these days. Like many people, I’m trying to address this, but the truth is that if I don’t know an artist and they haven’t been recommended to me by someone whose taste I trust, I need to be grabbed very quickly for something to make an impact on me.

On the flip side, once I am interested and do open that box, I’m disappointed if I find nothing else of substance inside. It’s one reason I left TikTok last year - I got tired of the superficial nature of everything - where I’d spend an hour scrolling and then struggle to tell you about a single video I saw afterwards.

So this idea of an album really appealed to me - one that is able to combine two opposites: quick transmission of ideas and rewarding deep exploration.

### Permission to curate

Finally one day it struck me that I could take what I this album represented to me, and use it as inspiration for my own work! (D’oh)

I had an epiphany - I already had a shedload of short track ideas! At least, I’d been calling them “track ideas”, but they could easily simply be called “tracks” if I wanted them to be. Skrillex did, after all.

I’ve always been this way - relying on other artists braver than me to blaze a trail, giving me permission to follow their lead and forge my own path.

I think this comes from imposter syndrome - feeling like I’m not a “proper” creative person, which I’ve [spoken about elsewhere](https://open.substack.com/pub/rachelconnor/p/episode-4-stories-of-creative-courage?utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&timestamp=970.8). So seeing other artists break the mould and do things differently really opens my mind.

It means I’m regularly redefining what it means to be an artist.

### Panning for gold

Getting excited about the idea that I already had an album among the piles of track ideas I’ve created, I started to sift through them more intentionally.

With so many ideas (I think I had around 450 at the time) where was I to begin? I didn’t want to dive back into the earlier ideas, back when I was still learning the software and developing my skills - they’d need too much work to get them sounding release-ready.

So, rather arbitrarily, I picked the ideas numbered between 300 and 399.

I sat down and listened through them all, earmarking my favourites. I was listening for anything that I liked the sound of. Not necessarily tracks that would go well together, just tracks that I wanted to see the light of day. Feeling audacious, I wanted as many tracks as possible at this stage (I definitely underestimated how long it would take to finish the album at this stage).

Next came the process of deciding what order to put them in. I sought guidance from [Spooqs](https://spooqs.space) on this, who had recently tackled a similar challenge of a mixed collection of tracks. I took the advice to order tracks based primarily on BPM rather than mood, as listeners can adjust to quick shifts in mood more easily than an abrupt shift in tempo. So, I ordered the tracks based on their tempo and didn’t think about moods fitting together - and I’m glad I did it this way. I found my tracks with similar tempos had complementary moods anyway, and where they didn’t, I felt that the changes kept the experience interesting and organic.

Once I got to this stage, I started to put all my tracks into one big Ableton set to work on them. This is where the fun exciting part started to slowly morph into something more challenging…

First, I quickly found that this was too much for my CPU to handle, and split this out into 3 separate projects instead.

Next, I found that the process of transitioning between tracks was going to be more challenging than I thought, despite them being similar tempos. I was aiming for the kind of seamless compositional transitions of great DJ sets - where elements of one track blend into the next and, for a moment, you’re experiencing a fascinating crossover of two worlds.

I realise now how much consideration, effort and trial and error this takes! I needed to change some of the tracks to fit together better, and I extended some tracks to make things seem slightly less frantic. I got there in the end, largely through brute force rather than any clever technique, and was happy with the way the tracks flowed together.

### Permission to complete

When working on a big project like this, it can be difficult to know when to stop. There is always more you could be adding, removing, changing, improving. When to call it is a real skill I’m learning to develop.

Even though my first EP was only 3 tracks, I spent *so long* on it - at least 30 versions of each track and I dread to think how many hours. By the end, I didn’t even like the tracks anymore, truly! But I’m no quitter, and I was pushing myself to the finish line through sheer willpower. At that stage, finishing the project was more important to me than enjoying it (however dark that sounds).

I had this in the back of my mind for this album project, and so I relied on certain methods to keep me motivated. I would [post updates to social media](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DMQU_Y4qEtI/?utm_source=ig_web_copy_link&igsh=MzRlODBiNWFlZA==) which I found heartening, but which took me too much time editing to keep this up. And when I did start to feel a glimmer of resentment for the tracks, or a fed up feeling when working on them, I knew that it was the perfect time to get some distance.

It was then that I sought feedback on the tracks from two friends and fellow producers, [Incertezza](https://linktr.ee/incertezza) and [Circul & Eas](https://circulandeas.com). It meant that I had the perfect excuse to not listen to it for a couple of weeks, and leave it in the hands of other people for a while. I used [Samply](http://samply.app) to send the album to them (as one big audio file) which allowed them to leave timestamped comments.

I found this reinvigorating - getting encouragement and critical feedback on things to work on next was just what I needed.

Finally, I worked with friend and mastering engineer [Matthew Hayter](https://matthewhaytermusic.com) on finishing touches and achieving commercial loudness.

Finally, it was ready to release. While I was working on the album I was inspired by [Flume’s 2019 release](https://soundcloud.com/flume/hi-this-is-flume-mixtape), and I followed his lead and decided to call my release a mixtape album.

At this stage, I let it sit for a few weeks.

I’d spent so much time working on the album, and I wanted to really honour that in the way that I shared it with the world. To be honest, I felt exhausted at this stage and wanted to get the album out and move on to other projects, but I knew I’d regret it if I didn’t do something meaningful to mark the release.

At a minimum, I knew I wanted to write this article to document the experience.

But I wanted to do more than that - I wanted to find a way to share what I’ve learned and valued throughout this process.

### Community, inspiration, and permission to call it art

Whether it was being inspired by Skrillex, Fred Again or Flume, seeking advice or feedback from Spooqs, Circul & Eas and Incertezza, or feeling encouraged by social media likes - I learned the importance of [community](https://www.metalabel.com/editorial/groupcore?srsltid=AfmBOorFmiQQAwyyBP4dbIJO9o_KlrCW_QMHJKV2hwQPl5aPpW7RBE5j) when working on creative projects. They say 2026 is the year of moving past self-care into [community care](https://www.healthline.com/health/when-self-care-becomes-community-care), and I’ve certainly experienced the value of community this year.

I also learned that you never know who you’re going to inspire with your contributions to the world. None of the people above necessarily know how much of an inspiration they are to me, and I’ve undoubtedly forgotten countless more inspirations that helped me create this album. The point being, to keep contributing your ideas to the world, because whether it’s now or some day in the future, you could inspire someone to do something that means a lot to them. You never know who you’re going to open the door for.

Finally, I learned that relinquishing control is good for me. I like things a certain way and I’m a bit of a control freak when it comes to my work and my art. I’ve usually got an image of a perfect end result in mind, which leads me to believe the best way to get there is to apply skill, intention and focus. But, thinking back to the origins of the album, they didn’t come from skill, intention or focus. They came from play. Making things for the fun of it. The intention and focus came later, but the beginning was all about giving up control and just having fun exploring. Learning how to edit, refine and find intentionality among a pile of artefacts.
